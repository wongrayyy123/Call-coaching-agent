# Legal / Procurement Rubric (v1 — Legal/procurement scorecard from Leo's gsheet)

Score each criterion 1-5 using the anchors below. Overall = arithmetic mean of the 8 scores, rounded to two decimals.

Source: [Leo's Legal / procurement scorecard tab](https://docs.google.com/spreadsheets/d/1ByqU5GE6L0_nL0gtmFXtJxsgHs8mkN1N-t1-3MWC4Js/edit).

Apply this rubric to **Legal/Procurement** calls — the AE driving contract redlines, security reviews, procurement workflows, and the final push to signature. NOT pricing/packaging discussions (Proposal). The scorer must read this file before scoring any Legal call. Output the canonical JSON shape at the bottom — the keys are stable across rubric revisions.

---

## 1. Owning a Written Mutual Close Plan

**Question:** How effectively did the rep drive a written mutual close plan with named owners, dates, and dependencies across each workstream?

- **1** — No close plan exists; the rep is reacting to events with no owners, dates, or sequencing, and is effectively letting legal/finance/the customer set the pace.
- **2** — A loose verbal sense of next steps exists but nothing is written, owned, or dated.
- **3** — A partial close plan exists but lacks named owners, dates, or coverage of all workstreams (legal, security, procurement).
- **4** — A written close plan with owners and dates exists for most workstreams and the rep is steering it, with minor gaps in dependencies or cadence.
- **5** — The rep is clearly driving a written mutual close plan (MAP) with named owners, dates, and dependencies for legal, security, and procurement — owning the close themselves rather than delegating it to legal, finance, or the customer.

**Answer guide:** Assess whether the rep owns the close via a written plan, per the playbook: 'AE owns the close plan - not legal, not finance, not the customer. Drive a written mutual close plan with named owners, dates, and dependencies for each workstream.' Rate 5 if a written MAP with owners/dates/dependencies covers all three workstreams and the rep is visibly steering it. Look for: 'here's our close plan — legal redlines with [name] by Tue, security pack with [name] in parallel, procurement form by Fri,' explicit ownership of the timeline. Rate 4 if a written plan covers most workstreams with minor gaps. Rate 3 if partial and missing owners/dates or a workstream. Rate 2 if next steps are only verbal. Rate 1 if there's no plan and the rep is reactive. Bad examples: tracking the deal in their head; deferring entirely to the customer's procurement timeline.

## 2. Running Legal, Security & Procurement in Parallel

**Question:** How effectively did the rep advance legal, security, and procurement workstreams in parallel rather than sequentially?

- **1** — Workstreams are run strictly in sequence (e.g. waiting for legal to finish before starting security), creating avoidable delay.
- **2** — The rep is aware of multiple workstreams but is effectively progressing only one at a time.
- **3** — Two workstreams are moving but the third is stalled or not yet started.
- **4** — All three workstreams are in motion but not fully synchronised; some dependencies are managed reactively.
- **5** — The rep has legal, security, and procurement advancing in parallel from the start, with dependencies sequenced deliberately to compress the timeline.

**Answer guide:** Evaluate parallelisation, per the playbook: 'Run legal, security, and procurement in parallel from day one' and 'in parallel, not in sequence.' Running serially is a primary cause of timeline slippage. Rate 5 if all three workstreams are progressing simultaneously with deliberate dependency management. Look for: 'while legal reviews redlines, let's get security the SOC 2 pack and start the vendor portal in parallel.' Rate 4 if all three are moving but loosely coordinated. Rate 3 if one workstream is stalled/unstarted. Rate 2 if effectively serial. Rate 1 if strictly sequential. Bad examples: 'we'll deal with security once the contract is signed'; holding procurement until legal is fully done.

## 3. Identifying Signing Authority & Procurement/Legal Contacts

**Question:** How effectively did the rep identify and engage the final signing authority and the named legal and procurement contacts?

- **1** — Signing authority and procurement/legal contacts are unknown; the rep is working through the champion alone with no named contacts.
- **2** — Contacts are referenced generically ('their legal team') but none are named or engaged.
- **3** — Some contacts are named but signing authority is unclear or unconfirmed, or the rep hasn't engaged them.
- **4** — Signing authority and most contacts are identified and partially engaged, with minor uncertainty.
- **5** — The rep has identified and engaged the named legal and procurement contacts and confirmed the final signing authority and their availability — removing ambiguity about who drives and who signs.

**Answer guide:** Assess clarity on who drives and signs, per the playbook entry criteria ('procurement and legal stakeholders are identified, and we have an introduction or clear path to them') and key questions ('who has final signing authority and what's their availability?'), and the red flag 'signing authority unclear or shifts mid-cycle.' The training emphasised identifying the procurement contact and the lawyer by name rather than 'never spoke to.' Rate 5 if named legal/procurement contacts are engaged and signing authority and availability are confirmed. Look for: 'who's the lawyer reviewing this, and can we get 15 minutes with them?', 'who signs, and are they available before quarter-end?' Rate 4 if most are identified and partially engaged. Rate 3 if some named but authority unclear. Rate 2 if only generic references. Rate 1 if unknown. Bad examples: relying solely on the champion with no contact in legal/procurement; not knowing who ultimately signs.

## 4. Proactive Security Handling

**Question:** How effectively did the rep handle the security review — providing artifacts proactively and controlling questionnaire scope?

- **1** — The rep is reactive on security, waiting to be asked, with no proactive sharing and no control over expanding scope.
- **2** — The rep provided some security material only after being prompted and did not manage scope.
- **3** — The rep shared security artifacts but did not get ahead of the review or address scope creep.
- **4** — The rep proactively shared most artifacts and managed scope reasonably, with minor lapses.
- **5** — The rep proactively sent the security pack (SOC 2 Type II, DPA, pen test summary) before being asked, used the response bank to move fast, and actively managed/pushed back on expanding questionnaire scope — escalating to Mustakeem where needed.

**Answer guide:** Evaluate proactive security management, per the playbook: 'Send the security pack proactively (SOC 2, DPA, pen test summary) before they ask,' the assets (SOC 2 Type II report, security questionnaire response bank), the escalation path ('escalate... to Mustakeem (security)'), and the red flag 'security questionnaire scope keeps expanding.' Rate 5 if the rep proactively provides artifacts, leverages the response bank, and controls scope (escalating to Mustakeem as needed). Look for: 'I've already sent over our SOC 2 Type II and DPA so security isn't a blocker,' pushing back when the questionnaire expands beyond scope. Rate 4 if mostly proactive with minor lapses. Rate 3 if artifacts shared but reactively. Rate 2 if shared only when prompted. Rate 1 if purely reactive with no scope control. Bad examples: waiting for the security questionnaire before mentioning SOC 2; letting scope balloon without challenge.

## 5. Contract Redline Management & Paper Choice

**Question:** How effectively did the rep manage contract redlines — pushing for Encord's paper where possible, looping in legal fast, and refusing to accept 'stuck in legal'?

- **1** — The rep is passive on redlines, accepts 'it's with legal' as a status, and has no visibility into what's outstanding or why; defaulted to the customer's paper without reason.
- **2** — The rep is tracking redlines loosely but is not driving them and tolerates vague legal status.
- **3** — The rep is engaged on redlines but slow to loop in internal legal, or hasn't challenged the choice of paper/the 'stuck in legal' status.
- **4** — The rep is managing redlines actively, looped in legal, and is mostly on top of outstanding items, with minor gaps.
- **5** — The rep drove redlines actively — looping in Maira via #gtm-legals the moment they arrived, pushing for Encord's MSA where reasonable, getting the lawyer's name, and offering a live redline call rather than accepting 'stuck in legal.'

**Answer guide:** Assess redline ownership, per the playbook: 'Loop in Maira via #gtm-legals the moment redlines arrive,' 'Never accept 'it's stuck in legal' as a status - get the lawyer's name and offer a 15-min redline call,' key question 'are we using Encord's MSA or their paper?', and the red flag 'customer insists on their paper without a clear reason.' Rate 5 if the rep loops in Maira/#gtm-legals immediately, pushes for Encord's MSA where reasonable, knows the lawyer, and offers a live redline call. Look for: 'I've sent the redlines to our legal team today,' 'can we get your counsel on a 15-minute call to close these out?', a clear rationale on whose paper. Rate 4 if actively managed with minor gaps. Rate 3 if engaged but slow to escalate internally or unchallenged status. Rate 2 if tracking loosely. Rate 1 if passive and accepts 'stuck in legal.' Bad examples: accepting the customer's paper with no reason; reporting 'still in legal' week over week with no action.

## 6. Compelling Event & Close Date

**Question:** How effectively did the rep confirm and reinforce a target close date and the compelling reason to close within that window?

- **1** — No target close date and no compelling event; the deal has no deadline driving it.
- **2** — A date is mentioned but it's arbitrary, with no underlying business reason to hit it.
- **3** — A target date exists but the compelling event is weak or not reinforced during the call.
- **4** — A target close date and a reasonable compelling event are established and mostly reinforced, with minor gaps.
- **5** — The rep confirmed a target close date anchored to a genuine compelling event (e.g. a model launch, budget cycle, project go-live) and reinforced why the prospect needs to close within that window — using it to drive urgency across workstreams.

**Answer guide:** Evaluate whether a real deadline drives the close, per the playbook entry criterion 'a target close date is agreed,' the key question on procurement timeline and 'holiday/quarter-end risks,' and the training point that by this stage the team 'should have a very clear line of sight as to why they need to make something happen in the next four weeks.' The red flag 'procurement not engaged until final week of quarter' signals a missing/weak compelling event. Rate 5 if a target close date is tied to a genuine compelling event and reinforced to drive urgency. Look for: 'to hit your model launch, we need signature by the 30th — here's what that requires from each side.' Rate 4 if a date and reasonable event are set with minor gaps. Rate 3 if a date exists but the event is weak/unreinforced. Rate 2 if the date is arbitrary. Rate 1 if neither exists. Bad examples: 'let's aim to wrap up soon' with no date or reason; a close date with no business driver behind it.

## 7. Leveraging the Champion & Exec Sponsor to Unblock

**Question:** How effectively did the rep mobilise the champion and/or exec sponsor to push redlines, security, and procurement internally?

- **1** — The rep is trying to push the process entirely alone and is not using the champion/sponsor, who appears disengaged.
- **2** — The champion is in the loop but the rep is not actively enlisting them to unblock workstreams.
- **3** — The rep leans on the champion occasionally but not systematically across the stalled workstreams.
- **4** — The rep is using the champion to help unblock most workstreams, with minor underuse of internal leverage.
- **5** — The rep is actively mobilising the champion/exec sponsor to push redlines, expedite security, and navigate procurement internally — using insiders who know the org and can move things the rep cannot — and is meeting key stakeholders in person for high-value deals where warranted.

**Answer guide:** Assess use of internal leverage, per the playbook ('escalate... externally to your champion or exec sponsor') and the training, which stressed leaning 'very, very heavily' on the champion in this stage because they 'work within the same building... can do things to push this deal forward that you cannot,' and meeting in person for high-value deals ('for 210K in legal we should go and meet these people in person'). The red flag is 'champion goes quiet during redlines.' Rate 5 if the rep is systematically mobilising the champion/sponsor to unblock workstreams (and meeting in person for high-value deals where appropriate). Look for: 'can you nudge your legal team on the redlines?', 'who internally can expedite the vendor setup?', proposing an onsite for a large deal. Rate 4 if the champion helps unblock most workstreams. Rate 3 if used occasionally. Rate 2 if the champion is informed but not enlisted. Rate 1 if the rep goes it alone with a disengaged champion. Bad examples: not having spoken to the champion in over a week on a high-value deal; trying to push procurement entirely over email/Slack when an in-person meeting would open it up.

## 8. Maintaining Momentum & Escalating Stalls

**Question:** How effectively did the rep maintain a weekly cadence and escalate stalled workstreams (no silence), both internally and externally?

- **1** — The deal has gone quiet (e.g. no engagement for over a week) with no escalation; the rep is sitting on their hands.
- **2** — Contact is sporadic and the rep tolerates stalls without escalating.
- **3** — The rep maintains some cadence but does not escalate workstreams that have stalled beyond a few days.
- **4** — The rep keeps a near-weekly cadence and escalates most stalls, with minor lapses in timeliness.
- **5** — The rep maintains at least a weekly cadence with the buyer, never goes silent, and escalates any workstream stalled more than ~5 business days — internally to Maira (legal) or Mustakeem (security) and externally to the champion/exec sponsor.

**Answer guide:** Evaluate cadence and escalation discipline, per the playbook: 'Weekly cadence with the buyer is the floor, not the ceiling. Don't go silent. If a workstream stalls more than 5 business days, escalate - internally to Maira (legal) or Mustakeem (security), and externally to your champion or exec sponsor.' The training flagged 'no engagement after... 11 days' and 'partially complete after 3 weeks' as serious problems. Rate 5 if the rep sustains at least weekly contact, never goes silent, and escalates stalls (>~5 business days) on both sides. Look for: an explicit weekly check-in, 'it's been stuck for a week — I'm escalating to our legal lead and looping your sponsor,' proactive follow-up. Rate 4 if near-weekly with most stalls escalated. Rate 3 if some cadence but no escalation of stalls. Rate 2 if sporadic with tolerated stalls. Rate 1 if the deal has gone silent with no escalation. Bad examples: a redline outstanding 11 days with no nudge; waiting weeks on a partially complete security questionnaire without escalating.
